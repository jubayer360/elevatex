Section 01 · The Two Enemies

Why your brain lets go, and why re-reading does not save it

Before any AI tool can help, you have to understand the two things working against you: a brain built to forget, and a study habit that feels like learning but is not.

The CurveWhat you keep if you do nothing

Hermann Ebbinghaus measured this in 1885. Learn something once, do nothing with it, and most of it is gone within a week. The exact numbers vary by person and material, but the shape is brutally consistent.

You did not forget because you are a weak student. You forgot because that is what an unrehearsed memory does. The whole game is what you do to pull it back out.

ENEMY 01

The forgetting curve

Memory fades fast without use. One pass through a chapter is the start of forgetting, not the end of learning. The fix is to keep retrieving it as it tries to fade.

ENEMY 02

The illusion of competence

Re-reading and highlighting make material feel familiar. Familiar feels like known, but it is not. You can recognise an answer on the page and still be unable to produce it on a blank one.

THE FIX 01

The testing effect

Pulling information out of your head builds memory far more than putting it in again. Every successful recall, especially a hard one, strengthens the path. This is the most useful finding in this booklet.

THE FIX 02

The blank page test

The only honest measure of learning. Close everything and write what you know from memory. What survives the blank page is yours. What does not, you only ever recognised.

Mini Glossary
Retrieval practice
Studying by recalling from memory rather than re-reading. The engine behind almost everything here.
Active recall
The everyday name for retrieval practice: quiz yourself, do not just review.
Illusion of competence
Mistaking the feeling of familiarity for real understanding. The reason cramming feels like it worked.
The Line to Remember Re-reading is recognition. Retrieval is learning. So make the AI test you, not teach you.

Section 02 · The Mindset Shift

Stop letting AI finish your thinking

Most students use AI to get answers. The best students use it to get questions. That single difference decides whether you walk out of an exam confident or blank, even after the same hours of work.

9:47 PM

Riya, Six Weeks Later

Same desk, same cold cup of cha, but now there are four textbooks instead of one. Four midterms in nine days: Statistics, Consumer Behaviour, Marketing Management, Microeconomics. The one she has been hiding from since week one is Statistics with Professor Rahman. She types explain hypothesis testing, reads a flawless four paragraph answer, thinks okay, I get it, and closes the laptop. Ten minutes later she cannot tell her roommate what a p-value is. She did not learn anything. She watched the AI know something.

The One Sentence An answer engine finishes your thinking for you. A study partner makes you do the thinking, and refuses to let you skip it. The first one feels productive and fades in a week. The second one feels like effort, and stays.
Answer Engine you receive
  • You ask it to explain, it explains, you nod along.
  • It feels fast and productive while you read.
  • The information goes in and slides back out within days.
  • The work builds the model's understanding, not yours.
  • You meet the same question in the exam as a stranger.
Study Partner you retrieve
  • You ask it to quiz you, it asks, you struggle, you answer.
  • It feels effortful, which is exactly the point.
  • Pulling the answer out is what burns it into memory.
  • The work builds your understanding, every single time.
  • You meet the exam question as something you have already beaten.

Science anchor A well-prompted AI tutor with active engagement produced larger learning gains than an actively taught lecture in the same time (Kestin et al., 2024, Harvard physics). The model is not the bottleneck. The rules are.
The Big Idea · Bloom's 2 Sigma In 1984 a researcher named Benjamin Bloom found that students with a one to one tutor scored two standard deviations higher than students in a normal classroom, enough to move an average student into the top two percent. For forty years nobody could scale it, because human tutors are rare and expensive. You are the first generation that can have a patient, subject expert tutor at three in the morning, for free. That problem just got solved, and almost nobody in your class knows it yet.

Section 03 · Worked Example

The same topic, asked two ways

Riya types two requests into the same chat box, five minutes apart. One asks the AI to explain. One forbids it from explaining and tells it to teach instead. Watch what happens to her brain.

Before

The answer engine

What Riya Typedexplain hypothesis testing and p-values
"Hypothesis testing is a formal procedure used in statistics to evaluate assumptions about a population. It begins with a null hypothesis and an alternative hypothesis. The p-value represents the probability of observing results as extreme as those measured, assuming the null hypothesis is true..."

Result: four flawless paragraphs she reads, nods at, and forgets within ten minutes. She built nothing. She watched the AI know something.

After

Professor Sage

What Riya Rebuilt[TEACH ME AS] You are Professor Sage, a patient stats tutor. I am a BBA student, weak at maths, scared of this, exam in 9 days. Topic: hypothesis testing and p-values. [USE THIS METHOD] One idea at a time, analogy before formula. [TEST ME, DON'T TELL ME] Ask me one question, wait, tell me if I am right and why, then ask the next. Do not lecture. [OWN MY WEAK SPOTS] Re-test what I miss. [REMEMBER & RESUME] End with a recap. Begin now.
"Great, let's start simple, no formulas yet. Imagine you flip a coin 10 times and get 9 heads. Would you start to suspect the coin is rigged? Just answer yes or no, and tell me why in one line."

Result: she has to think. She answers, gets corrected, climbs one rung at a time. By the end she can explain a p-value in her own words. That is learning, not reading.

The Lesson Same machine. Same five minutes. The only difference is one instruction: make me retrieve, do not let me receive. A scared student just acquired a private, patient professor who works at midnight and never sighs. The gap between the two columns is the gap between a chatbot user and a student who actually learns.

Section 04 · The Tutor Setup

Five blocks that turn a chatbot into a teacher

A tutor is not a one off task, it is a relationship. You should not re-describe yourself every session. Write this setup once, reuse it all semester. Day 1 gave you CRAFT-RIO for tasks. This is TUTOR, for learning. The letters spell what it does.

T
Teach me as
Who the tutor is, plus who you are: your subject, your real level, your goal and your exam date. This is what stops you getting a generic robot.
U
Use this method
The pedagogy. One idea at a time, an analogy before any formula, examples from real life, and a check that you understood before moving on.
T
Test me, don't tell me
The most important line in this booklet. Quiz me, make me explain it back, give hints before answers, and never hand over the solution until I have tried.
O
Own my weak spots
Track what I keep getting wrong and re-test it later. Name my misconceptions directly. If I get everything right, make it harder.
R
Remember & resume
End each session with a short recap I can paste back next time, so we pick up where we stopped instead of starting over. A saved game.

Skeleton
Copy this and swap in any subject you study

TUTOR Skeleton[TEACH ME AS] You are a patient ... tutor. I am a ... student, my level is ... , my exam is in ... days. The topic is ... . [USE THIS METHOD] One concept at a time. Give a plain analogy before any formula. Use real life examples. Check I understood before moving on. [TEST ME, DON'T TELL ME] Default to asking, not telling. One question at a time. When I am wrong, ask a simpler leading question instead of correcting me. Only explain fully after I have genuinely tried. [OWN MY WEAK SPOTS] Keep a running list of what I get wrong and re-test it later. Name any misconception directly. If I get everything right, get harder. [REMEMBER & RESUME] At the end, write a short recap: what we covered, what I nailed, what I struggled with, and the first question for next time. Begin now with your first question.
Why It Works This is the Description skill from Day 1, aimed at behaviour rather than output. You are not describing a document you want, you are describing a teacher you want. That move is called Performance Description, and it is one of the most underused prompt skills there is.

Section 05 · The Science

Five laws the top students study by

AI is the tool. These five laws are how you point it. Each law comes with the exact instruction you give your tutor. Use them and you study like the top one percent, because this is what they actually do.

LAW 01

Retrieval beats review

Pulling it out builds memory more than putting it back in. The single highest value instruction you can give: quiz me from memory before you ever explain.

LAW 02

Spread it out

Five short sessions across five days beat one long cram. The forgetting between them is the workout. Ask for a spaced plan that re-tests old topics on later days.

LAW 03

Teach it to learn it

If you cannot explain it simply, you do not own it. Flip roles: I will explain this like you are 12, you catch every place I go vague or wrong.

LAW 04

Mix it up

Studying one topic in a smooth block feels good and teaches little. Mixing forces your brain to choose the right tool. Ask to quiz me across all my subjects in random order.

LAW 05

If it feels easy, stop

Smooth, comfortable studying is usually just review of what you know. Struggle is the signal. Tell it: do not make this comfortable, if I get two right in a row, get harder.

ONE WARNING

A tutor can still lie

Everything from Day 1 still holds. The AI can quiz you on a wrong formula with total confidence. A tutor that hallucinates fails you in the exam. The next section fixes that completely.

Say It Out Loud Re-reading is recognition. Retrieval is learning. Make the AI test you, not teach you. Five laws, one principle.

Section 06 · PROBE Self-Test

Test yourself before the exam does

SPARK makes the tutor probe you. PROBE is how you test yourself when the chat is closed. Re-reading feels like learning; retrieval is learning.

P

Pause answers

No definitions until you attempt.

R

Recall blind

Quiz without notes visible.

O

Oral explain

AI lists gaps, not a lecture.

B

Bridge

Link today's topic to last week's.

E

Error hunt

Exam traps and misconceptions.

MASTER PROBE MODEEnter PROBE mode for [TOPIC]. Do not give definitions until I attempt. Quiz one question at a time. After 5 answers, score 0–2 each and name my weakest sub-topic.
Master PROBE instruction (pin this)Enter PROBE mode for [TOPIC]. Rules: P — Do not give definitions or solutions until I attempt first. R — Quiz me without my notes visible to me; I will answer from memory only. O — After each answer, list gaps in my explanation, not a full lecture. B — Ask one bridge question linking this topic to [PRIOR TOPIC]. E — End with three "exam traps" students fall into and how to avoid each. Start with: "Question 1 of 5 (recall only):"
Blind recall check (5 min)Quiz me on [TOPIC] with 5 questions. Difficulty: [intro / midterm / final]. Show one question at a time. Do not show answers until I reply "done" to all five. Then score 0–2 per question and name my weakest sub-topic.
Explain-it-back (oral-friendly)I will explain [CONCEPT] in my own words in 3–5 sentences. After I send it, respond with ONLY: - ✅ Accurate points (bullet list) - ❌ Errors or vague spots (bullet list) - 🔧 One analogy that fixes the biggest gap Do not rewrite my explanation for me.
Concept transfer (exam-grade)I learned [CONCEPT A] in class. Ask me to apply it to a new scenario: [SCENARIO YOU PROVIDE]. If I cannot, give a hint question, not the solution. After 2 hint questions, show a partial solution with the last step blank for me to complete.
Error hunt (before exam)For [TOPIC], list 7 common student misconceptions. For each: give a 1-question trap that exposes the misconception. Do not reveal which misconception until I answer wrong or right. After all 7, tell me which 2 I should drill tonight based on my wrong answers.
Illusion-of-knowing breakerI will paste my highlighted notes below. Your job: find 5 places where the notes look clear but a student might still fail an exam question. For each, write one application question that tests understanding, not recognition. NOTES: [PASTE]

Section 07 · PIPE + Production

Top students do not read more. They convert.

You will not read 200 pages of slides in nine days. You convert them into things you can be tested on, and you feed the AI your real material so it cannot make anything up.

Layer A: big picture · B: bullets · C: flashcards · D: mock quiz — then PROBE on Layer C only. Copy prompts below.

The Menu
What to generate, and the catch on each one

The Asset The Prompt Seed The Catch
Layered notes "Summarise this chapter in three layers: one sentence, one paragraph, one page." Check the one liner captures the real point.
Mind map "Give me a text mind map of how these concepts connect." You should be able to redraw it from memory.
Practice problems "Ten exam style problems, hardest last, answers hidden until I ask." Do them closed book, every time.
Mock quiz "A ten question mixed quiz. Mark it. Explain only what I got wrong." This is retrieval, not reading.
Flashcards "Turn this into 30 question and answer flashcards I can import into Anki." Test yourself, do not flip and nod.

Grounding
How to make sure the AI did not invent any of it

STEP 01

Feed it your sources

Upload your lecture slides, the textbook chapter, or a recording of the class. Tools like NotebookLM answer only from what you give them.

STEP 02

Check the footnotes

A grounded answer cites the exact line in your source. Click it. If a claim has no source in your material, treat it as a guess.

STEP 03

Then make it study material

From your own sources, generate a study guide, a mind map, even an audio overview you can listen to on the bus. Grounded, and free.

The Grounding Rule For anything you will be graded on, make the AI work from your sources, not its memory. Upload the lecture, and it cannot invent, because it is reading instead of remembering. That one habit separates students who get burned by AI from students who get carried by it.

Demo backup
Sample lecture snippet (Consumer Behavior)

Use if live upload fails during SAVE-GAME / PIPE demo.

Chapter 5 excerpt (simplified)**Topic:** Cognitive dissonance and post-purchase behavior (Chapter 5 excerpt, simplified for workshop) When a consumer makes a choice that conflicts with their beliefs or values, they experience psychological tension called cognitive dissonance. Festinger's theory (1957) predicts they will reduce tension by changing attitudes, adding consonant cognitions, or downplaying the importance of the conflict. Example: A student buys an expensive textbook they rarely open. They might tell friends "everyone buys it" (social validation) or focus on one chapter they used (selective evidence) rather than admit wasted money. In collectivist markets, dissonance may be resolved through group norms: family approval of a purchase can outweigh private regret. Campaigns like Share a Coke reduce dissonance by personalizing the product: the bottle feels chosen, not imposed. Exam angle: Dr. Hasan often asks you to apply dissonance to a **local brand decision**, not recite Festinger's biography. **Common trap:** Confusing dissonance with mere disappointment. Dissonance requires two conflicting cognitions held at once.
