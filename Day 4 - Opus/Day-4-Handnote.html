Decision When to use which

Need citations with sources?

Perplexity. Every claim links to where it came from. Use for salary research, market data, and any fact you need to verify before an interview.

Analyzing a long PDF or report?

Claude or Gemini. Claude's Projects feature lets you pin documents across conversations. Gemini 2.5 Pro handles up to 1 million tokens natively.

Writing and iterating drafts?

ChatGPT Canvas or Claude. Canvas lets you edit inline. Claude follows voice rules precisely. Both are strong for FORGE-style writing.

Quick Office integration?

Copilot. If you live in Microsoft 365 (Word, PowerPoint, Excel), Copilot writes where you work. No copy-paste between browser and document.

03
The Context Economy

Treat Context Like Money

Every AI model has a finite memory for each conversation. Power users spend that memory on purpose. Everyone else wastes it.

Key Terms
Token
A chunk of text (roughly 3/4 of an English word). "Bangladesh" = 3 tokens. "I want to prepare for my interview" = about 8 tokens. Models count everything in tokens: your input, their output, system instructions.
Context Window
The total number of tokens a model can hold in one conversation. Think of it as RAM, not storage. When it fills up, the model starts forgetting earlier parts of the conversation.
Input Tokens
What you send: your prompt, pasted documents, previous conversation history. All of it counts.
Output Tokens
What the model generates in response. This also uses the budget.

How tokens flow through a conversation. When the window fills, you must decide: compress or restart.

Problem Lost in the Middle

Research shows that models pay the most attention to the beginning and end of context. Information buried in the middle of a long conversation gets less attention. This is called the "Lost in the Middle" effect (Liu et al., 2023).

Put your most important instructions at the top and bottom. The middle is where things get lost.

Rules The 6 Commandments of Context

1
Load Order Matters

Put role and critical instructions first. Put reference material in the middle. Put the task and output format last.

2
Summarize Before Continuing

If a conversation passes 20 exchanges, ask: "Summarize our key decisions so far in bullet points." Then paste that summary into a new chat.

3
One Task, One Chat

Never mix CV drafting and interview prep in the same conversation. Each task gets its own context budget.

4
Delete Noise

Do not paste your entire thesis when you only need Chapter 3. Trim inputs to only what the model needs for this specific task.

5
Front-load Identity

Start every serious conversation with: who you are, what you need, and the constraints. Do not let the model guess.

6
Fresh Start > Fixing Drift

If the model starts giving weird answers after many exchanges, do not say "no, I meant..." five times. Copy the good parts. Start a new chat.

P-CONTEXT · Context window auditTASK: I am about to start a complex task. Before I begin, help me plan my context budget. MY TASK: [Describe what you want to accomplish] MATERIALS I WANT TO PASTE: 1. [Document/text 1 - estimate length] 2. [Document/text 2 - estimate length] 3. [Document/text 3 - estimate length] QUESTIONS: 1. Which of these materials are essential for this task? Which can I skip? 2. In what order should I present them for best results? 3. Should this be one conversation or should I split it into stages? 4. What is the single most important instruction I should put at the very end of my prompt? Help me spend my context budget wisely. No wasted tokens.
04
Markdown Prompting

Structure That Machines Parse

When you use markdown headers, bullet points, and clear labels in your prompts, AI models process them more accurately. This is not decoration. It is architecture.

AI models are trained on billions of markdown documents: GitHub READMEs, documentation pages, structured articles. When your prompt uses the same formatting, the model "knows where it is" in your instructions. A flat paragraph forces the model to guess your structure. Markdown eliminates that guessing.

Before vs. After

Before: Flat Paragraph

Wall of Text Prompt

I want you to act as a career coach and help me prepare for my Unilever marketing intern interview. I am a 3rd year BBA student at IBA Dhaka with experience in social media management for a campus club and I did a project on consumer behavior in rural Bangladesh. I want you to give me 5 likely interview questions and for each one give me a STAR format answer using my real experience and also tell me what mistakes to avoid and rate each answer 1-5 on clarity. Also at the end give me a 2-minute opening pitch.

The model sees one undifferentiated block. Role, context, evidence, task, output format, and constraints are all mixed together. The model has to parse intent from a paragraph. It often misses pieces.

After: Markdown Structured

Architected Prompt

# Role Career coach. Skeptical, evidence-focused. # My Profile - 3rd year BBA, IBA Dhaka - Social media manager, Business Club (2 semesters) - Consumer behavior project: rural Bangladesh (field survey, n=120) # Task 1. Generate 5 likely interview questions for Unilever Marketing Intern 2. For each: draft a STAR answer using ONLY my evidence above 3. For each: list 2 common mistakes to avoid 4. Rate each answer: Clarity 1-5, Evidence 1-5 # Output Format Table with columns: Question | STAR Answer | Mistakes | Clarity | Evidence # Constraints - No invented metrics - If evidence is thin, say "EVIDENCE GAP" instead of fabricating - End with a 2-minute opening pitch (max 180 words)

Every section is labeled. The model knows exactly what is context, what is task, and what format to use. Result: more complete, more accurate, fewer follow-up corrections.

Framework The Prompt Architecture Stack

LAYER 1

# Role

Who the model should be. One sentence. Be specific: "Hiring manager at a Dhaka FMCG firm" beats "helpful assistant."

LAYER 2

# Context / Profile

Your Learning Context Document, evidence bank, or the document being analyzed. Structured with bullet points.

LAYER 3

# Task

Numbered steps. Each step is one action. "Analyze, then compare, then recommend" = three numbered items.

LAYER 4

# Output Format + Constraints

Table, bullet list, JSON, or paragraph? Max length? Ban list? Put this last so the model remembers it (recency effect).

P-MARKDOWN · Markdown prompt template# Role [One sentence: who the model should be, what expertise, what tone] # Context - Background: [your situation] - Document: [paste or summarize the relevant material] - Constraints: [budget, timeline, location, ethics] # Task 1. [First action - be specific] 2. [Second action] 3. [Third action] # Output Format - Format: [table / bullets / numbered list / prose paragraph] - Length: [max word count or "keep it under one page"] - Language: [English / Bangla / mixed] # Rules - [Rule 1: e.g., "No invented statistics"] - [Rule 2: e.g., "Flag assumptions as ASSUMPTION"] - [Rule 3: e.g., "If you lack information, ask before guessing"]
05
JSON Prompting

Structured Input, Structured Output

When you need machine-readable data, tables you can paste into spreadsheets, or consistent output across multiple runs, use JSON schemas.

JSON (JavaScript Object Notation) is a way to organize data with clear keys and values. You do not need to be a programmer to use it. Think of it as a form with labeled fields that the AI fills out.

Before vs. After

Before: Vague Request

Unstructured Output

"Analyze this meeting and give me the key points, action items, and who is responsible for each." Result: A long paragraph mixing summaries, action items, and names. You spend 10 minutes reformatting it into something usable.
After: JSON Schema

Structured Output

Result: { "meeting_date": "2025-06-01", "attendees": ["Riya", "Tanvir", "Dr. Hasan"], "decisions": [ "Launch social media campaign by June 15" ], "action_items": [ { "task": "Draft Instagram content calendar", "owner": "Riya", "deadline": "2025-06-08", "status": "pending" } ] }

Example Meeting Notes Extractor Schema

JSON Schema Definition{ "meeting_summary": { "date": "YYYY-MM-DD", "duration_minutes": number, "attendees": ["string"], "purpose": "string (one sentence)" }, "key_decisions": [ { "decision": "string", "decided_by": "string", "rationale": "string (one sentence)" } ], "action_items": [ { "task": "string (specific, measurable)", "owner": "string", "deadline": "YYYY-MM-DD", "priority": "high | medium | low", "dependencies": ["string"] or [] } ], "open_questions": ["string"], "next_meeting": "YYYY-MM-DD or null" }
P-JSON · JSON structured prompt# Role Data extraction assistant. Precise, no invented fields. # Input [Paste your raw text: meeting notes, interview transcript, article, research data] # Schema Return output as valid JSON matching this exact structure: ```json { "summary": "string (max 2 sentences)", "key_points": [ { "point": "string", "evidence": "string (quote from input)", "confidence": "high | medium | low" } ], "action_items": [ { "task": "string", "owner": "string or UNASSIGNED", "deadline": "YYYY-MM-DD or TBD" } ], "gaps": ["string (what is missing from the input)"] } ``` # Rules - Every "evidence" field must be a direct quote from the input - If a field cannot be determined, use null, not a guess - Do not add fields not in the schema - Output ONLY the JSON, no commentary before or after
When to use JSON prompting Use JSON when you need: (1) consistent output you can compare across runs, (2) data you will paste into Excel or Google Sheets, (3) structured analysis of multiple documents, or (4) output that feeds into another prompt or tool. For creative writing or brainstorming, stick to markdown prompts.
06
Free Tier Maximization

Near-Paid Quality, Zero Cost

Most students assume free tiers are limited to basic models. The truth: free accounts on the major platforms can do 90% of what paid accounts do, if you use them correctly.

TACTIC 01

Rotate platforms

When ChatGPT free hits its GPT-4o limit, switch to Claude. When Claude's message cap resets, switch back. Stagger your usage across 3 platforms and you rarely hit walls.

TACTIC 02

Use Gemini for long documents

Gemini's free tier gives you access to models with massive context windows. Upload your 50-page report there instead of paying for Claude Pro.

TACTIC 03

Perplexity for all research

5 Pro searches per day plus unlimited quick searches. Use Pro searches for complex research. Use quick for simple fact-checking. Never waste a Pro search on a simple question.

TACTIC 04

Write prompts offline first

Draft and refine your prompt in a text editor before pasting it. One well-crafted prompt beats five iterative "no, I meant..." messages that eat your free tier quota.

TACTIC 05

Save and reuse prompt templates

Keep a Google Doc or Notion page with your best prompts (FORGE, STAGE, RADAR). Copy, customize the brackets, paste. Reusing good prompts is free.

TACTIC 06

Smaller models for small tasks

Do not use GPT-4o to fix a typo or reformat a list. Use the lighter model (GPT-4o mini, Gemini Flash). Save the powerful model for tasks that actually need reasoning.

TACTIC 07

Use Custom Instructions / System Prompts

ChatGPT's custom instructions persist across all conversations. Set your Learning Context Document there once. Every new chat starts with your identity pre-loaded, for free.

TACTIC 08

Time your usage

Free tier limits often reset at midnight UTC (6 AM Dhaka). Heavy usage in the morning Bangladesh time means you get a fresh quota while competitors have already burned theirs.

Ethical Foundation

Your AI Collaboration Policy

The 1% user does not hide AI use or use it blindly. They have a written policy that states what AI does, what they do, and where the line is. This policy protects you in academic settings, job interviews, and client work.

01
What AI does for me Be specific about the role AI plays in your workflow
ExampleStructures outlines, generates first drafts from my evidence, checks grammar, suggests alternatives. I use ChatGPT for drafting and Claude for analysis.
02
What I always do myself Non-negotiable human tasks
ExampleSupply all facts and evidence. Make every strategic decision. Edit every line to match my voice. Verify every claim. Take full responsibility for the output.
03
What I never let AI do Hard boundaries
ExampleInvent statistics or credentials. Write personal statements without my real stories. Make ethical decisions for me. Replace my judgment on anything I sign my name to.
04
How I disclose AI use Transparency protocol
ExampleIn academic work: I note "AI-assisted drafting" in my methods section. In job applications: if asked, I explain my evidence-first FORGE process. I never claim AI output as purely my own creation.
05
My verification checklist Steps before any AI output goes public
ExampleRead aloud test. Check every number against source. Run ban-list scan. Ask: "Could I defend this sentence in a phone call?"

Sustained Growth

Quarterly Self-Review

AI tools change every month. New models launch. Features appear and disappear. The 1% user builds a habit of reviewing and upgrading their approach every quarter.

Review 1
Which platform did I use most? Was it because it was best for my tasks, or just habit?
Review 2
Which prompt template saved me the most time? Can I improve it?
Review 3
Did I catch any AI error that would have embarrassed me? What was my catch rate?

The Quarterly Review Structure

AUDIT

Platform audit

Review chat history. Count how many conversations you started on each platform. Check: did you use the right tool for each job, or did you default?

SKILLS

Prompting skills check

Compare a prompt you wrote 3 months ago to one you write today. Is it more structured? Does it include constraints? Are you using markdown sections?

ERRORS

Error log review

What mistakes did AI make that you caught? What mistakes did AI make that you missed and only found later? Build your personal "things to double-check" list.

UPDATE

Tool landscape scan

Did any platform launch new features? Did a new model release? Update your decision matrix. Add new tools to your rotation if they fit.

P-GROWTH · Quarterly AI review# Role Personal AI productivity coach. Data-driven, no flattery. # My Usage Summary - Platforms I used this quarter: [list] - Main tasks: [list typical tasks] - Biggest win (where AI saved me real time): [describe] - Biggest failure (where AI output was wrong or useless): [describe] - New features I tried: [list or "none"] # Task 1. Diagnose: Am I in a platform rut (using one tool for everything)? 2. Score my prompting growth: compare the sample below to good practice [Paste one prompt you used this quarter] 3. Recommend 2 specific features I should try next quarter 4. Suggest 1 prompt template I should build for a recurring task 5. Give me one metric to track next quarter (e.g., "time saved per week" or "errors caught before submission") # Output Quarterly report card format. Scores 1-5 for: Platform diversity, Prompt quality, Error awareness, Feature adoption. Total score /20.

Build Your Personal AI OS

Your AI Operating System is one document that contains everything you need to use AI effectively. Build it now. Use it for the rest of your career.

  1. Platform Map (3 min) - List your 3 go-to platforms and what you use each for
  2. Custom Instructions (5 min) - Paste your Learning Context Document into ChatGPT's custom instructions. Set up a Claude Project if available.
  3. Prompt Template Library (5 min) - Copy P-MARKDOWN and P-JSON into a Google Doc. Add your favorite prompts from Day 1-3 (CRAFT-RIO, FORGE, STAGE).
  4. Decision Matrix (3 min) - Fill in your personal version: "For [task type], I open [platform] because [reason]."
  5. AI Collaboration Policy (5 min) - Use P-POLICY to generate your draft. Edit it to match your values. Save it permanently.
  6. First Quarterly Review Date (1 min) - Set a calendar reminder for 90 days from today to run P-GROWTH.
  7. Test Drive (8 min) - Take one real task (interview prep, assignment, email) and run it through your OS: pick platform, structure prompt with markdown, execute, verify output.
