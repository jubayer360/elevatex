Section 01 · Mental Model

How the machine actually decides what to say

Before any prompting technique can help you, you need to know what is happening behind the chat box. Skip this and every other section will sit on sand.

11:04 PM

Riya, the Case Study, and the Chat Box

Riya Karim, third year BBA marketing student in Dhaka. A 1,500 word case study on Coca Cola's Share a Coke campaign is due in ten hours. Her professor, Dr. Hasan, dislikes buzzwords and rewards specific evidence. She types into ChatGPT: write me a 1500 word analysis of coca cola share a coke campaign. What comes back is fluent, polite, and forgettable. Why it failed is everything you learn today.

The One Sentence A large language model is a probability machine. Given everything written so far, it guesses the most likely next chunk of text. Then the next. Until it decides to stop.

Four Facts
Carry these into every future chat

FACT 01

Tokens, not words

The model reads text as chunks called tokens, not letters. This is why it can draft a polished essay yet miscount the R's in strawberry.

FACT 02

No memory by default

Every new chat starts blank. The model does not know your name, your subject, or yesterday's conversation. Day 02 fixes this.

FACT 03

A knowledge cutoff

Training stopped on a specific day in the past. Anything fresh, regional, or niche is suspect by default.

FACT 04

Confidence is not correctness

The model is built to sound certain. It will back wrong answers with perfect grammar. This is hallucination. Section 07 covers it.

DemoWhy strawberry breaks it

Ask: how many R's are in strawberry? Many versions answer two. There are three. The model sees two chunks, not nine letters.

straw berry

Two tokens. Counting letters inside them is not its default move.

Mini Glossary
Token
The chunk of text the model reads and writes at a time.
Context Window
How much text the model can hold in working memory at once.
Hallucination
Stating something false with full confidence, often with invented sources.

Section 02 · The Fluency Framework

Four habits that separate operators from users

Most students use AI on instinct. The four habits below replace instinct with method. The framework is published by Anthropic and is the spine of every other technique in this booklet.

How do I own the final result?

D 01  ·  DELEGATION

What to ask AI in the first place

Problem awareness. Name the actual goal. Not "write 1500 words" but "earn an A from Dr. Hasan".

Platform awareness. Pick the right tool. Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity are not interchangeable.

Task split. Decide which parts you write, which parts AI drafts, and which parts you do together.

D 02  ·  DESCRIPTION

How to tell it what you want

Product. Format, length, structure, tone of the output itself.

Process. How the model should think. Step by step, with counter arguments, by drafting three versions.

Performance. How the model should behave. As a strict editor, a patient tutor, a tough interviewer.

D 03  ·  DISCERNMENT

How to judge the answer

Product. Is the output accurate, specific, and in your voice?

Process. Did the reasoning skip a step or accept a false premise?

Performance. Did the model behave the way you asked, or did it default to bland?

D 04  ·  DILIGENCE

How to own the outcome

Creation. Was this the right tool, and was the chat appropriate to the task?

Transparency. Did you disclose AI assistance where you should have?

Deployment. Did you fact check every number, quote, and citation before you submitted?

Quiet Confession Most students are weakest at Description and Diligence. Description because they treat the chat like Google and type three words. Diligence because they trust the first answer. Fix those two and your work changes inside a week.

Section 03 · Prompt Anatomy

Eight building blocks of a prompt that works

A strong prompt is not longer. It is thicker. Every line earns its place. The acronym below is a checklist, not a script. Use what fits the task and skip what does not.

C
Context
Who you are, what the situation is, what came before. Without this the model defaults to generic.
R
Role
Who the model should be while answering. Strict editor, patient tutor, sharp business journalist.
A
Action
The single verb. Outline. Critique. Translate. Summarise. Do one thing well, not five things poorly.
F
Format
Structure of the output. Sections, headings, length, bullets, table, list of three.
T
Tone
Voice and register. Direct, analytical, allergic to jargon, written like a journalist not a textbook.
R
Reasoning
How the model should think. Step by step, list three options, argue against itself, then choose.
I
Input
Reference material you provide. Notes, the paragraph you wrote, the rubric, the job description.
O
Output Rules
Hard limits. Banned phrases, claims marked unverified, end with three sharpening questions.

Skeleton
Copy this template and adapt it to any task

CRAFT-RIO Skeleton[CONTEXT] I am ... . The situation is ... . What came before is ... . [ROLE] Act as ... with experience in ... . [ACTION] Do exactly one thing: ... . Do not ... yet. [FORMAT] Structure the answer as: ... . [TONE] Write like ... . Avoid sounding like ... . [REASONING] Before answering, think step by step: 1. ... 2. ... 3. ... [INPUT] Here is the material you should use: """ ... """ [OUTPUT RULES] Do not use these phrases: ... . Mark any claim you are unsure about with [VERIFY]. End by asking me three sharpening questions before you draft anything longer.
Rule of Thumb A good prompt reads like a job advertisement. Who you want. What they should do. With which materials. What the output should look like. What is forbidden. If your prompt cannot fill out that ad, you are not ready to hit enter.

Section 04 · Worked Example

Same question. Same AI. Different worlds.

Two prompts written five minutes apart, into the same chat box. One pays rent on every line. The other types as if Google were on the other side. Compare the outputs and the lesson is permanent.

Before

The lazy prompt

What Riya Typed at 11:04 PMwrite me a 1500 word analysis of coca cola share a coke campaign
"Coca Cola's Share a Coke campaign was a remarkably successful global marketing initiative that effectively used personalisation to engage consumers in today's fast paced beverage industry. By replacing the iconic logo with popular first names, the company built emotional resonance and tapped into the universal human desire for individual recognition..."

Result: fifteen hundred words of correct, polite, forgettable text. Dr. Hasan circles four buzzwords on page one and writes a B at the top.

After

The CRAFT-RIO prompt

What Riya Rebuilt at 11:11 PM[CONTEXT] I am a 3rd year BBA marketing student in Dhaka. Dr. Hasan dislikes generic buzzwords and rewards specific evidence, named theories, and unconventional angles. [ROLE] Act as a brand strategy lecturer who has graded 500 student essays. [ACTION] Build an outline for an essay on Coca Cola's Share a Coke campaign (Australia 2011, global 2014). Do not write the essay yet. [FORMAT] Thesis sentence + 3 sections with sub claims + counter argument + open conclusion. [TONE] Sharp business journalist, no jargon. [REASONING] Before writing, decide: 1. What makes it academically interesting? 2. What is the strongest counter argument? 3. Which theory frames it best (Kotler, Aaker, Holt, Sharp) and why? [OUTPUT RULES] No "synergy", "leverage", "resonance". Mark uncertain claims with [VERIFY]. End by asking me 3 sharpening questions.
"Thesis: Share a Coke succeeded not because of personalisation but because it converted a private bottle into a public social object. Section 1: identity transfer ... Section 2: scarcity engineering (150 names, 18 million Australians) ... Counter argument: Sharp's How Brands Grow predicts ..."

Result: a sharp scaffold with three sub claims, nine evidence hooks, a counter argument, and three questions Riya has to answer herself. She still writes the essay. The grade rises a full letter.

The Lesson The AI did not write Riya's essay. Riya still wrote it. The AI was the most expensive private tutor in history, and it was free. The difference between the two outputs sitting above is the difference between a chatbot user and an operator. The gap is your prompt.

Section 05 · Your Homepage

Write this once. Paste it forever.

The Learning Context Document is one short paragraph about you that you keep in a note, a Google Doc, or your phone. Every new chat begins with it. From that point on, the model knows your major, your professor's pet peeves, and the voice you are trying to develop.

01
Who I am, in one sentence.Age, year, university, one thing you are working toward.
Riya's AnswerRiya Karim, 21, third year BBA student in Dhaka, aiming at FMCG brand management.
02
What I study and where.Major, expected graduation.
Riya's AnswerBBA, Marketing major, graduating June 2027. Strong in Consumer Behaviour, weak in Statistics.
03
My career direction, even if it is a rough guess.One or two target roles or industries.
Riya's AnswerBrand management at Unilever Bangladesh or BAT, then regional roles in South Asia.
04
My language background and how it shapes my writing.Native language, writing habits to fix.
Riya's AnswerBangla native. English defaults to long sentences and formal words. Help me cut both.
05
The writing voice I am developing.Three adjectives that describe your target voice.
Riya's AnswerSpecific. Slightly journalistic. Never corporate.
06
What my professors penalise.Three concrete things, not vague feelings.
Riya's AnswerBuzzwords. Claims with no numbers. Conclusions that repeat the intro.
07
What my professors reward.Three concrete habits you have seen earn high marks.
Riya's AnswerNamed frameworks. Local plus global examples. Real counter arguments.
08
My personal line on AI use, in one sentence.What you will and will not let AI do for you.
Riya's AnswerAI for outlines, critique, and fact checking. The final words must be mine.
Where to Keep It Paste this paragraph into ChatGPT Custom Instructions, Claude Project Instructions, or Gemini Saved Info. On a free account, keep it in a Notes app and paste at the top of every new chat. Update once a semester.

Section 06 · Two Modes

Hand it over, or sit beside it

Two modes. Pick the wrong one and AI either wastes your time or quietly takes credit for the part of your degree you were supposed to develop yourself.

Automation AI does it
  • Stakes are low and the cost of a small mistake is small.
  • The task is repetitive, mechanical, or pattern based.
  • You can verify the output in under a minute.
  • Your voice is not the point of the work.
  • You would have outsourced this to an intern if you had one.
Augmentation AI sits beside you
  • Stakes are high or the work counts toward a grade.
  • Original thinking, judgement, or argument is involved.
  • Your voice and reasoning are the point of the work.
  • You want to learn the topic, not just produce the deliverable.
  • A teacher or employer will ask you to defend it later.

Drill
How to classify common student tasks

The Task Verdict Why
Polite email to reschedule a dentist appointment Automate No voice required, low stakes.
The opening paragraph of a case study you will be graded on Augment Voice is the point. Have AI critique, not draft.
Summarising a 40 page lecture PDF for revision Automate Pattern work. Spot check five claims afterwards.
Choosing the thesis of your essay Augment This is the thinking. Owning the thesis is owning the grade.
Translating Bangla notes into clean English Automate Mechanical, easy to verify.
Generating 50 flashcards from a chapter Automate Bulk task. Verify a random five.
Replying to a sensitive message from a close friend Neither Stay out. Real relationships need full presence.
Writing a eulogy for your grandmother Never Some work is sacred. It is not AI's to touch.

Before You Submit
Three checkpoints to clear, every time

Truth Check
Is anything in here factually wrong? Did I verify every number, quote, and citation?
Voice Check
Does this sound like me, or does it sound like AI doing an impression of a polite stranger?
Stake Check
If my professor, boss, or parent asked me to defend any sentence right now, could I?

Section 07 · Diligence

The most dangerous thing AI does

It is not the obvious wrong answer. It is the confidently wrong answer, written in perfect grammar, with sources that sound real. Catching it is a skill, and the skill is built on three habits.

Hallucination
Three situations where the model invents the most

TRIGGER 01

Citations and sources

Papers, page numbers, book quotes, DOIs. The model writes plausible authors and journals that do not exist. Always verify before citing.

TRIGGER 02

After the cutoff date

Recent news, fresh product launches, last week's prices. The model improvises confidently. Use a web search tool instead.

TRIGGER 03

Niche or local knowledge

Small companies, regional events, Bangladeshi figures, lesser known places. The model leans on guesswork. Treat output as a draft until confirmed.

Disclosure
When to mention AI in your work

Red
Do not submit as your own work

AI wrote the essay, the cover letter, or the reflection. You lightly edited. This is academic dishonesty in almost every policy worldwide. Example: you typed "write me 1500 words" and submitted what came back with three words changed.

Amber
Heavily AI assisted, so disclose clearly

You did the thinking. AI helped with outline, brainstorming, structure, or chunks of drafting. Add a short note in a footnote or acknowledgement. Example: "Outline developed with Claude; all writing my own. Conclusion drafted in collaboration and revised three times."

Green
Lightly AI assisted, no disclosure needed

AI helped the way a thesaurus, spell checker, or tutor would. The thinking and writing are unmistakably yours. Examples: a synonym suggestion, a grammar correction, an explanation of a concept that you then wrote up in your own words.

The Eleven Word Test If I had to defend every line of this in front of the person who graded it, could I? If yes, submit. If no, you are not the human in the loop, you are the rubber stamp.

Section 08 · Practice

Tonight's practice. Tomorrow's habit.

Read everything in this booklet twice and you still will not learn it. Try the three exercises below today, and you will.

Three Prompts
Try one of these before you sleep tonight

01 · Rebuild an old chat
10 min
Open ChatGPT or Claude. Find a lazy prompt you typed in the last two weeks. Using the CRAFT-RIO skeleton in Section 03, rebuild it. Run both. Compare the two outputs.
02 · Install your context
5 min
Type out the eight answers from Section 05 into a single note on your phone. Paste it at the top of three new chats tomorrow. Notice how the answers change.
03 · Catch a confident lie
15 min
Ask the model: "Give me three peer reviewed papers about [your topic] with authors, journals, and DOIs." Google the first one. If nothing comes up, you have caught your first hallucination.
